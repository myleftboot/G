Central nervous system

  • The brain and spinal cord make up the central nervous system (CNS). The CNS is linked to sense organs by nerves
  • Cells called receptors in sense organs detect stimuli (changes):
    • eyes – light
    • ears – sound, position, balance
    • tongue and nose – taste and smell
    • skin – touch, pressure, pain, temperature
  • A light receptor cell has a nucleus and a cell membrane which contains cytoplasm.

Neurones

  • Nerve impulses are transmitted along cells called neurones. A large bundle of neurones is called a nerve.
  • A receptor cell responds to a stimulus by sending an electrical impulse along a sensory neurone to the CNS.
  • The information in the impulses is coordinated by the brain or spinal cord and an impulse is sent along a motor neurone to effector organs (muscles or glands).

Structure of neurones (Edexcel)

  • Neurones are long, thin cells with a cell body containing the nucleus. At one end, the dendron carries impulses from a sense receptor or another neurone to the cell body. The dendron has many branches to make contact with receptors.
  • From the cell body, the long thin axon carries the impulse to the other end of the neurone.
  • The axon is protected by a fatty coating called a myelin sheath. This also makes nerve impulses travel faster.

Reflexes

  • A reflex action is a response to a stimulus that does not involve the brain. It is faster than a conscious reaction because you don’t have to think about it:
    1. An electrical impulse travels from a sense receptor along a sensory neurone to the spinal cord.
    2. The impulse jumps across a tiny gap called a synapse to a relay neurone in the spinal cord.
    3. The relay neurone passes the impulse across another synapse to a motor neurone.
    4. The motor neurone transmits the impulse to an effector. The effector may be a muscle, which responds by contracting, or a gland, which releases a chemical.
  • This process is called a reflex arc. If you tread barefoot on something sharp, a muscle in your leg contracts and quickly lifts your foot away without you needing to think about it.

Synapses

  • The electrical nerve impulses are carried across the gaps – the synapses synapses – between neurones by chemicals called neurotransmitters.
  • Edexcel: Neurotransmitters are released at the ends of the axon of one neurone: they cross the gap and attach to receptors on the dendrites of the next neurone. This causes an electrical impulse to travel along the second neurone.
