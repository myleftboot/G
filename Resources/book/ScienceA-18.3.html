Paying for electricity

  • Energy from the mains supply is measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh) (see Section 18.2). Each unit of electricity is 1 kWh.
  • The kilowatt-hours (units) used is recorded by an electricity meter.
  • Energy companies charge for each kilowatt-hour used. Total cost = number of kWh used × cost per kWh.

    Example
    These are two readings from an electricity meter taken 3 months apart.

    The energy company charges 14p per kWh. What is the cost in pounds of the energy used?

    energy used = 46 800 – 45 300
    = 1500 kWh
    cost = 1500 × 14p = 21 000p
    = £210

    The cost of the energy used is £210.

  • The cost of running an appliance can be worked out from its power in kilowatts, the time it is switched on in hours, and the cost per kilowatt-hour. cost (p) = power (kW) × time (h) × cost per kWh (p/kWh)

    Example
    An energy company charges 15p per kilowatt-hour. How much will it cost to use a 3 kW electric kettle 12 times for 5 minutes each time?
    The cost of using the kettle is 45p.

Saving energy

  • An electrical device that is more efficient transfers more electrical energy to useful energy (see Section 17.X). [refers to AQA P1.2]
  • A more efficient device costs less to run for the same amount of useful energy.
  • Old-style filament light bulbs waste energy by getting hot. They are being replaced by more efficient compact fluorescent lamps (CFL) and light-emitting diode (LED) lamps.
  • The table compares different types of GU10 spotlight bulbs with the same brightness:
    TypePowerCost
    halogen 35 W £1.95
    CFL 7 W £6.75
    LED 4 W £12.20
    Example
    A spotlight bulb is on 8 hours per day. Electricity costs 15p per kWh. How much will you save using an LED bulb compared with a halogen bulb over 4 weeks?

    time lamp is on = 4 × 7 × 8 = 224 h
    cost for halogen = 0.035 × 224 × 15
    = 117.6p
    cost for LED = 0.004 × 224 × 15
    = 13.44p
    saving = 117.6 – 13.44 = 104.16p

    The saving is 104p, or £1.04, over 4 weeks.

Payback time and cost efficiency (AQA, Edexcel)

  • Choosing a more efficient electrical device saves on energy costs. But the initial cost of buying it may be high.
  • Payback time is the length of time it takes to save the money it costs to buy the item.

    Example
    You decide to replace a broken GU10 bulb with a CFL bulb (£6.75) that will save 24p each week compared with a halogen bulb (£1.95). What is the payback time?

    greater cost of CFL = £6.75 – £1.95
    = £4.80
    saving per week = 24p
    payback time = 480 ÷ 24 = 20 weeks

    The payback time is 20 weeks.

  • Energy can be saved in the home by using low-energy devices, and also by insulating the home. The most cost efficient, or cost effective, method is the one that saves the most money compared with its cost in the shortest time.

    Example


    Which of the two methods of insulation is the more cost effective?

    compare payback times:
    cavity wall insulation
    350 ÷ 100 = 3.5 years
    loft insulation
    200 ÷ 150 = 1.33 years

    Loft insulation is more cost effective.

Choosing appliances (AQA, Edexcel)

  • Factors to consider when choosing an electrical appliance:
      what you need it to do
    • how well it does the job required
    • purchase cost
    • running costs (energy use, repairs)
    • environmental costs.
  • Energy-saving products have both advantages and disadvantages:
    • a CFL lamp uses less energy than a filament lamp, but is more expensive to buy and takes some time to reach full brightness.
    • a microwave oven uses less energy than a conventional oven but cannot make food crisp and brown.
  • Electrical appliances have energy ratings from A++ (most efficient) to G (least efficient). Fridges, freezers, washing machines and dishwashers have A+++ as the highest rating.
