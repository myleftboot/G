Extremophiles (AQA)

  • Organisms that have extreme adaptations are called extremophiles. For example, at low temperatures the giant groundsel (see Section 4.1) produces an anti-freeze type chemical: this stops water in its cells from freezing and damaging the cells.
  • Extreme characteristics are often found in simple organisms like bacteria: these characteristics take less evolutionary time to develop (see Section X.X) than they would in complex organisms.
  • Pompeii worms live near deep ocean vents thousands of metres under the sea. These vents release mineral-rich water at temperatures of 80°C or more. The water pressure at this depth can be 100 times that of the surface.
  • The bacteria that Pompeii worms feed on use chemosynthesis to obtain nutrients from the vents. They have a mechanism that converts chemicals like hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and carbon dioxide (CO2) into simple carbohydrates, a basic food.

Parasitism and mutualism (Edexcel)

  • Some animals, plants and microorganisms live close to each other because one or both of them use the other to survive. The relationships can work in a variety ways, e.g. between animals and animals, animals and plants, microbes and animals, etc.
  • A parasitic relationship is where one organism benefits from another ‘host’ organism – often obtaining food – and in some way harms the host. For example, human head lice and tapeworms may live on us or in us (respectively), and are harmful: we function better without them
  • The parasitic relationship only benefits the parasite. For example, mistletoe grows on host trees, obtaining nutrients and water from them by inserting a special root into the host’s branches. The host gets nothing in return, and may even die.
  • In mutualism the relationship benefits both organisms. For example, oxpeckers feed on the blood-sucking insects that live on many African animals. This is food for the oxpecker and is useful to the animal; its skin is damaged by the insect parasites.
  • Another example of a mutually beneficial relationship is between nitrogen-fixing bacteria and legume plants such as peas, beans and soy. The bacteria live inside the legume’s roots.
  • In this case the relationship is more than just ‘useful’ – it is essential to the survival of both organisms. The plant provides the bacteria with food; the bacteria provides the plant with nitrogen compounds, which are essential for the plant’s growth.
