Chemical equations

  • In all chemical reactions, the atoms in the reactants rearrange to make new products. The products are new combinations of the atoms; they have different physical and chemical properties from the reactants.
  • Total mass before and after a reaction is the same (conservation of mass), no atoms are made or destroyed. Product mass is difficult to measure if one of the products is a gas; it may escape into the air.
  • Word equations describe the chemicals involved in a reaction. Their usefulness is limited: they do not say how much of each reactant/product is present, and they are written only in one language.
  • Symbol equations show exactly how much of the reactants and products are involved in a chemical reaction. These symbols are internationally recognised and understood.
  • Symbol equations must always balance, with the same number of each atom on each side of the equation. They should also show the state of each product: solid (s), liquid (l), aqueous solution (aq), or gas (g).

    Example balancing of an equation
    When tin oxide is heated with hydrogen, these form tin and water vapour. The unbalanced equation shows 2 oxygen atoms on the left, 1 on the right:

    SnO2 + H2 ⇒ Sn + H2O

    We balance any unbalanced parts first; we double the oxygen atoms by doubling the water molecules:

    SnO2 + H2 ⇒ Sn + 2H2O

    But this gives an unbalanced left side: the right side has 4 hydrogens, the left has 2. We double the hydrogen atoms on the left:

    SnO2(s) + 2H2(g) ⇒ Sn(s) + 2H2O(g)

    We must also add the physical states.

Tests for CO2, H2, O2 (Edexcel)

  • Test for carbon dioxide (also AQA): Limewater (calcium hydroxide) becomes cloudy/milky in the presence of carbon dioxide. The milky substance is white insoluble calcium carbonate:

    Ca(OH)2(aq) + CO2(g) ⇒
    CaCO3(s) + H2O(l)

  • Test for hydrogen: A lighted splint will ‘pop’ with a small squeaky explosion, if held in a test tube that contains hydrogen mixed with air.
  • Test for oxygen: A glowing splint (i.e. lit but then blown out) will relight vigorously if put in a test tube that contains oxygen.

Reactions of calcium compounds (Edexcel/AQA)

  • Calcium oxide combines with water in a vigorous reaction, initially making a white solid; this dissolves to make limewater as more water is added:

    CaO(s) + H2O(aq) ⇒ Ca(OH)2(s)

  • Neutralisation reactions: calcium oxide (CaO), calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)2) and calcium carbonate (CaCO3) are alkalis, and therefore neutralise acids. They have practical uses in farming to treat acidic soils.
  • Coal in power stations burns to release sulfur dioxide, an acidic gas that contributes to acid rain:

    S(s) + O2(g) ⇒ SO2(g)

    Calcium carbonate is used in power stations to prevent this; it reacts with and neutralises the acidic gases.
