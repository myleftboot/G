A healthy diet (AQA)

  • A healthy diet contains minerals, vitamins, fibre, carbohydrate, protein, fat, and water.
  • Together these provide a balanced diet, giving energy, building new cells, protecting us against disease.
  • An unbalanced diet results in malnourishment, which may lead to illness, e.g. deficiency diseases, which are caused by lack of minerals and vitamins.

Our energy needs vary (AQA)

  • Food gives us energy to live and move. The more exercise we do, the more food we need (and vice versa).
  • Our energy needs vary according to our age, sex, location. We generally need more energy if we are young, or pregnant, or live in a cold climate.
  • Muscle burns more energy than fat. Exercise gives us more muscle mass; athletes therefore need more energy.
  • Exercise is not always athletic; vigorous household chores and walking are also exercise.

Our metabolic rates vary (AQA)

  • Metabolic rate is the rate at which chemical reactions occur in cells of the body.
  • Metabolic rate increases with exercise, and in colder climates, and with higher muscle content in the body.
  • Two people who do the same amount of exercise and are the same sex, age, and weight may have different metabolic rates: their metabolic rate is partly inherited from their parents.
  • Men usually have a higher metabolic rate than women because they usually have proportionately more muscle than fat.

Our weight (AQA)

  • If our food gives us more energy than we use, the extra energy is stored in our bodies as fat. We can become overweight, or even obese.
  • Being overweight increases our risk of heart disease, high blood pressure, arthritis and type 2 diabetes (excessively high blood sugar levels).
  • However, some fat is essential to a balanced diet: it is a store of energy for ‘emergency’ use and cushions our organs.
  • We can look slimmer by exercising more and through slimming regimes.
  • ‘Slimming’ and ‘losing weight’ - exercise may help us to slim, at the same time our weight may not change much: exercise builds muscle; muscle weighs more than fat.
  • In some parts of the world drought, war and pests mean that people’s food gives them less energy than they need, and/or less food of the right type, leading to malnourishment.
  • Malnourishment can mean both eating too much and eating too little (of the right foods at the right amount). The term ‘weight problem’ therefore has two meanings.

Diabetes (Edexcel)

  • Diabetes is a disease where the sufferer has difficulty controlling their blood glucose levels. This is caused by a lack of insulin, an important hormone in the blood.
  • High blood glucose can cause abnormal urination, thirst and tiredness.
  • Type 1 diabetes can be controlled by injections of insulin into the sufferer’s subcutaneous fat: the fat easily absorbs insulin and moves it into the patient’s blood.
  • Type 2 diabetes is diagnosed when a patient’s cells are less responsive to insulin than they should be (more resistant to it), even if insulin is being produced (this occurs in the pancreas).
  • Type 2 diabetes is thought to be caused by modern lifestyles - high fat diets, lack of exercise, obesity- and by ageing. It is usually characterised by high blood glucose levels.
