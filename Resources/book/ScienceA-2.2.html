Hormones

  • Special glands in the body produce chemicals called hormones. They are released by the glands into the bloodstream and transported to other parts of the body.
  • Hormones control many of the processes that occur in cells and organs

The menstrual cycle

  • The menstrual cycle in women lasts about 28 days. Once a month an egg is released by one of the ovaries. It travels down the fallopian tube and the lining of the womb gets thicker.
  • If the egg is fertilised by a sperm, it embeds itself in the thickened lining of the womb. The woman is pregnant.
  • If the egg is not fertilised, the womb lining and the dead egg are flushed out of the body. This part of the cycle is the monthly period or bleed.

Hormones and the menstrual cycle

  • The menstrual cycle is controlled by several different hormones. They work together.
  • The pituitary gland in the brain releases follicle stimulating hormone(FSH). This causes one or more eggs in the ovaries to mature.
  • FSH also causes the ovaries to produce and secrete another hormone, oestrogen. Oestrogen stimulates the lining of the womb to become thicker.
  • The oestrogen also acts on the pituitary gland, slowing down production of FSH and stimulating production of another hormone, luteinising hormone (LH). LH acts on the ovaries. It stimulates the ovary to release a mature egg.

Oral contraceptives

  • Contraception means preventing pregnancy (birth control). An oral contraceptive contraceptive is a pill that contains female hormones. These hormones inhibit production of FSH by the pituitary gland. This prevents eggs in the ovaries from maturing.
  • The first oral contraceptive pills contained a lot of oestrogen and often had bad side effects. Newer contraceptives contain oestrogen plus another hormone, progesterone, and have fewer side effects.
  • Pills containing only progesterone have the lowest risk of side effects, but may not work so well.

Fertility treatment

  • Sometimes it is difficult for a woman to become pregnant. This may be because her body does not produce enough FSH.
  • A ‘fertility drug’ contains artificial FSH. This stimulates the eggs in the ovaries to mature. It also increases oestrogen production by the ovaries.
  • Sometimes the problem is caused by blocked or damaged fallopian tubes:
    even though mature eggs are released, they do not reach the womb. In vitro fertilisation (IVF) may help:
    1. The woman is given a fertility drug containing FSH and LH. This causes several eggs to mature at the same time.
    2. The mature eggs are removed from the ovary. They are mixed in a Petri dish with sperm collected from the father. Fertilisation occurs
    3. The fertilised eggs develop into embryos.
    4. When the embryos have become tiny balls of cells, one or two are put directly into the woman’s uterus (womb), where they may develop into a baby.
  • IVF is expensive, and it may take several attempts before a pregnancy results, if at all. But it has helped many otherwise childless couples to have children.
