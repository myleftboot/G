New ways of extracting metals

  • Ores rich in metals that are useful to us are becoming scarce. For example, it is increasingly difficult to find new sources of copper ores that are rich in copper, even though copper is a major part of the Earth’s crust.
  • Mining for metal ores also has considerable environmental impacts. New methods of metal extraction are therefore being developed.
  • Phytomining copper: This new technique extracts copper from plants. Some plants are good at absorbing copper from low-grade copper ores. They may be grown on heaps containing this ore type. They are then burned. Copper is extracted from the resulting ash by electrolysis.
  • Bioleaching copper: Another new (but very slow) technology uses bacteria to produce solutions called leachates, which contain copper compounds. As with phytomining the bacteria feed on low-grade copper ores. Electrolysis is used to extract the copper metal from the leachate.

  • However, when mixed with small amounts of carbon to make steel, iron becomes much stronger – the layers now find it more difficult to slide over each other:
  • The same principle applies to other alloys. For example, wedding rings are often made of a gold alloy, e.g. gold mixed with copper. The small percentage of added copper strengthens the ring for everyday use.
  • Edexcel: Gold of 100% purity is called 24-carat gold; at 75% purity it is 18-carat gold, and so on, in direct proportion to the other metals added
  • Edexcel: Similarly, the fineness of gold is a proportional measure of its purity, in parts per thousand. For example, gold at a fineness of 960 is 96% pure – approximately 23 carats.
  • Edexcel: So called ‘smart’ alloys, also called shape-memory alloys, are being developed for new and amazing uses. One example is nitinol, a nickel-titanium alloy. Its shape can be altered when cooled; its original shape returns when it is warmed up.

Recycling metals

  • Ever increasing percentages of metals are being recycled. The amount being recycled depends on all of us as individuals, businesses and governments.
  • For example, we can separate out metals like drinks cans and dispose of them at a collection point or as separate rubbish; or, we may simply mix them with other waste that ends up in a landfill site (see Life Cycle Assessment, Section 10.3).
  • Advantages and disadvantages of recycling metals:
    AdvantagesDisadvantages
    The remaining reserves of ores rich in metals will last longer if we mine them less… The energy used and cost of recycling (collecting, sorting, processing) need to be factored in to our overall assessment of recycling’s benefits.
    … and the environmental impact of mining ores, especially those that are harder to reach, will be reduced. Some metals are more expensive (financially speaking) to recycle than to continue extracting.
    Less energy is needed to recycle metals than to extract them: sometimes 95% less! This is a significant cost saving. Recycling techniques may produce other waste by-products.
    Recycling’s lower energy needs reduces CO2 emissions: electrolysis of some metal ores uses electricity, which is largely produced using fossil fuels.
